\section{Deterministic Finite Automata}
% ==============================================================================

A \textbf{Deterministic Finite Automaton} is a machine that reads a string of characters one
symbol at a time and decides whether to \emph{accept} or \emph{reject} it.  It is called
\emph{deterministic} because at every step there is exactly one possible next state ---
no guessing.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Definition (DFA).}  A \emph{deterministic finite automaton}
$\dfa=(Q,\Sigma,\delta,q_0,F)$ consists of:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item A finite set of \emph{states} $Q$
  \item A finite set of \emph{input symbols} $\Sigma$ (the alphabet)
  \item A \emph{transition function} $\delta\colon Q\times\Sigma\to Q$
  \item An \emph{initial state} $q_0\in Q$
  \item A set of \emph{accepting states} $F\subseteq Q$
\end{enumerate}
\end{defbox}

\subsection{Representing a DFA}

A DFA can be described in two equivalent ways:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Transition diagram} --- a directed graph where nodes are states, edges are
        labelled with symbols, the start state has an incoming arrow, and accepting states are
        drawn with double circles.
  \item \textbf{Transition table} --- rows are states, columns are symbols, each cell gives
        the next state.  Start state marked $\to$, accepting states with $*$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{example}
Consider the DFA with $Q=\{q_0,q_1,q_2\}$, $\Sigma=\{0,1\}$, start state $q_0$,
$F=\{q_1\}$, and transitions $\delta(q_0,0)=q_2$, $\delta(q_0,1)=q_0$,
$\delta(q_2,0)=q_2$, $\delta(q_2,1)=q_1$, $\delta(q_1,0)=\delta(q_1,1)=q_1$.

This DFA accepts any binary string containing the substring \texttt{01}.

\bigskip
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=2.8cm, on grid, auto]
  \node[state]                         (q0) {$q_0$};
  \node[state, right=of q0]            (q2) {$q_2$};
  \node[state, accepting, right=of q2] (q1) {$q_1$};
  \startarrow{q0}
  \path[->]
    (q0) edge[loop above] node[lbl] {$1$}   (q0)
    (q0) edge             node[lbl] {$0$}   (q2)
    (q2) edge[loop above] node[lbl] {$0$}   (q2)
    (q2) edge             node[lbl] {$1$}   (q1)
    (q1) edge[loop right] node[lbl] {$0,1$} (q1);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\bigskip\textbf{Transition table:}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|cc}
  \toprule
             & $0$   & $1$   \\
  \midrule
  $\to q_0$  & $q_2$ & $q_0$ \\
  $*\;q_1$   & $q_1$ & $q_1$ \\
  $q_2$      & $q_2$ & $q_1$ \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{example}

\section{Formal Languages}
% ==============================================================================

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Key definitions.}
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Alphabet} $\Sigma$ --- any finite set of symbols, e.g.\ $\{0,1\}$ or
        $\{a,b\}$.
  \item \textbf{Word} --- any finite sequence of symbols from $\Sigma$, including the
        \emph{empty word} $\varepsilon$.
  \item $\Sigma^*$ --- the set of all possible words:
        $\{\varepsilon,0,1,00,01,10,11,\ldots\}$
  \item \textbf{Language} $L$ --- any subset $L\subseteq\Sigma^*$.
\end{itemize}
\end{defbox}

\subsection{Word Length}

The \emph{length} of a word is defined inductively:
\[
  |w| \;:=\;
  \begin{cases}
    0     & \text{if }w=\varepsilon \\
    |v|+1 & \text{if }w=av,\ a\in\Sigma,\ v\in\Sigma^*
  \end{cases}
\]
The \emph{occurrence count} of symbol $a$ in word $w$ is written $|w|_a$.

\subsection{Example Languages over $\{0,1\}$}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
  \toprule
  Language & Description \\
  \midrule
  $L_1=\{x01y\mid x,y\in\Sigma^*\}$           & Words containing \texttt{01} as a substring \\
  $L_2=\{w\mid|w|=2^n\text{ for some }n\}$    & Words whose length is a power of $2$ \\
  $L_3=\{w\mid|w|_0=|w|_1\}$                  & Words with equal numbers of 0s and 1s \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{insightbox}
\textbf{Tip.}  Languages can be classified by complexity --- this is the \textbf{Chomsky
Hierarchy}.  The simplest class, \textbf{regular languages}, are exactly those recognisable
by DFAs and describable by regular expressions.
\end{insightbox}

\section{The Language Accepted by a DFA}
% ==============================================================================

A DFA processes a word by starting at $q_0$ and following transitions one symbol at a time.
After reading the last symbol, if the current state is in $F$ the word is \emph{accepted};
otherwise it is \emph{rejected}.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Definition.}  The \emph{language accepted} by $\dfa$ is:
\[
  L(\dfa)\;:=\;
  \bigl\{w\in\Sigma^*\;\big|\;
  \text{the run of }w\text{ in }\dfa\text{ ends in some }q\in F\bigr\}
\]
\end{defbox}

\begin{example}
Tracing $w=0010$ through the DFA from Section~2:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
  \toprule
  Step & Symbol & Current State & Next State \\
  \midrule
  Start & --- & $q_0$ & --- \\
  1 & $0$ & $q_0$ & $q_2$ \\
  2 & $0$ & $q_2$ & $q_2$ \\
  3 & $1$ & $q_2$ & $q_1$ \\
  4 & $0$ & $q_1$ & $q_1$ \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Final state $q_1\in F$, so $w=0010$ is \textbf{accepted}.
\end{example}

\section{DFA Processing \& Design}
% ==============================================================================

The central design question: \emph{what information does the machine need to remember in
order to make the correct accept/reject decision?}  Each state encodes a piece of that memory.

\subsection{Pattern: Words ending with \texttt{ab}}

Track the last one or two characters seen.  States: ``haven't seen useful prefix'',
``just saw \texttt{a}'', ``just saw \texttt{ab}''.

\section{The Extended Transition Function}
% ==============================================================================

The standard $\delta$ handles one symbol at a time.  The \textbf{extended transition
function} $\hatdelta\colon Q\times\Sigma^*\to Q$ generalises this to entire words.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Definition.}  Define $\hatdelta$ inductively:
\[
  \hatdelta(q,w)\;:=\;
  \begin{cases}
    q & \text{if }w=\varepsilon \\
    \delta\!\bigl(\hatdelta(q,x),\,a\bigr)
      & \text{if }w=xa,\ a\in\Sigma,\ x\in\Sigma^*
  \end{cases}
\]
\end{defbox}

This lets us write the accepted language as
$L(\dfa)=\{w\in\Sigma^*\mid\hatdelta(q_0,w)\in F\}$.

\begin{example}
For a DFA with states $\{1,2,3\}$, $\Sigma=\{a,b\}$, start state~$1$, $F=\{3\}$,
computing $\hatdelta(1,baba)$:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cll}
  \toprule
  Step & Computation & Result \\
  \midrule
  1 & $\hatdelta(1,\varepsilon)$                             & $1$ \\
  2 & $\delta(\hatdelta(1,\varepsilon),b)=\delta(1,b)$       & $1$ \\
  3 & $\delta(\hatdelta(1,b),a)=\delta(1,a)$                 & $2$ \\
  4 & $\delta(\hatdelta(1,ba),b)=\delta(2,b)$                & $3$ \\
  5 & $\delta(\hatdelta(1,bab),a)=\delta(3,a)$               & $3\in F$\;\checkmark \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Word $baba$ is \textbf{accepted}.
\end{example}

\section{Product Automata}
% ==============================================================================

Given two DFAs $\dfa^{(1)}$ and $\dfa^{(2)}$, we can build a single DFA for their
\emph{intersection} or \emph{union} by running both simultaneously.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Definition (Product Automaton).}
For DFAs $\dfa^{(1)}=(Q^{(1)},\Sigma,\delta^{(1)},q_0^{(1)},F^{(1)})$ and
$\dfa^{(2)}=(Q^{(2)},\Sigma,\delta^{(2)},q_0^{(2)},F^{(2)})$,
define $\dfa=(Q,\Sigma,\delta,q_0,F)$ by:
\begin{align*}
  Q &:= Q^{(1)}\times Q^{(2)} \\
  \delta\bigl((p,q),a\bigr) &:= \bigl(\delta^{(1)}(p,a),\;\delta^{(2)}(q,a)\bigr) \\
  q_0 &:= (q_0^{(1)},q_0^{(2)})
\end{align*}
The choice of $F$ determines the operation:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
  \toprule
  Operation & Accepting states $F$ \\
  \midrule
  Intersection $\cap$ & $F^{(1)}\times F^{(2)}$ \\
  Union $\cup$        & $(F^{(1)}\times Q^{(2)})\cup(Q^{(1)}\times F^{(2)})$ \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{defbox}

\subsection{Complement}

To build a DFA for $\overline{L(\dfa)}=\Sigma^*\setminus L(\dfa)$, simply \textbf{swap
accepting and non-accepting states} --- everything else stays the same.

\begin{insightbox}
\textbf{Tip.}  When building a product automaton, start from $(q_0^{(1)},q_0^{(2)})$ and
only trace reachable pairs --- unreachable states can be ignored.
\end{insightbox}

\section{NFAs \& Determinization}
% ==============================================================================

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Definition (NFA).}  A \emph{nondeterministic finite automaton} has the same 5-tuple
as a DFA, except the transition function maps to the \emph{power set}:
\[
  \delta\colon Q\times\Sigma\to\mathcal{P}(Q)
\]
A word is accepted if \emph{at least one} path through the NFA ends in an accepting state.
\end{defbox}

\begin{example}
The NFA below accepts strings that end in \texttt{ab}.  From state~1, reading \texttt{a}
nondeterministically leads to $\{1\}$ or $\{1,2\}$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=2.8cm, on grid, auto]
  \node[state]                        (1) {$1$};
  \node[state, right=of 1]            (2) {$2$};
  \node[state, accepting, right=of 2] (3) {$3$};
  \startarrow{1}
  \path[->]
    (1) edge[loop above] node[lbl] {$a,b$} (1)
    (1) edge             node[lbl] {$a$}   (2)
    (2) edge             node[lbl] {$b$}   (3);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
\end{example}

\subsection{The Power Set Construction}

Every NFA can be converted to an equivalent DFA.  The states of the new DFA are
\emph{subsets} of the NFA's states.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Construction (Power Set Automaton).}
Given NFA $\dfa=(Q,\Sigma,\delta,q_0,F)$, define
$\dfa^D=(Q^D,\Sigma,\delta^D,q_0^D,F^D)$ by:
\begin{align*}
  Q^D           &:= \mathcal{P}(Q) \\
  \delta^D(S,a) &:= \bigcup_{q\in S}\,\delta(q,a) \\
  q_0^D         &:= \{q_0\} \\
  F^D           &:= \{S\subseteq Q\mid S\cap F\neq\emptyset\}
\end{align*}
\end{defbox}

\begin{example}
NFA: $Q=\{1,2,3\}$, $\Sigma=\{a,b\}$, $q_0=1$, $F=\{3\}$, with $\delta(1,a)=\{1,2\}$,
$\delta(1,b)=\{1\}$, $\delta(2,b)=\{3\}$, all others $=\emptyset$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
  \toprule
  State         & $a$       & $b$       & Accepting? \\
  \midrule
  $\to\{1\}$    & $\{1,2\}$ & $\{1\}$   & No \\
  $\{1,2\}$     & $\{1,2\}$ & $\{1,3\}$ & No \\
  $*\;\{1,3\}$  & $\{1,2\}$ & $\{1\}$   & Yes ($3\in F$) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\bigskip
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[node distance=3cm, on grid, auto]
  \node[state]                        (A) {$\{1\}$};
  \node[state, right=of A]            (B) {$\{1,2\}$};
  \node[state, accepting, right=of B] (C) {$\{1,3\}$};
  \startarrow{A}
  \path[->]
    (A) edge[loop above]                     node[lbl]        {$b$} (A)
    (A) edge[bend left=15]                   node[lbl, above] {$a$} (B)
    (B) edge[loop above]                     node[lbl]        {$a$} (B)
    (B) edge[bend left=15]                   node[lbl, above] {$b$} (C)
    (C) edge[bend left=15]                   node[lbl, below] {$a$} (B)
    (C) edge[out=210, in=330, looseness=0.45] node[lbl, below] {$b$} (A);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
\end{example}

\begin{insightbox}
\textbf{Key fact.}  In principle $|Q^D|=2^{|Q|}$, but most subsets are unreachable in
practice.  Always build the table incrementally starting from $\{q_0\}$.
\end{insightbox}

\section{Regular Expressions}
% ==============================================================================

Regular expressions are a concise algebraic way to \emph{describe} regular languages.

\subsection{Operations on Languages}

\begin{defbox}
Three fundamental operations:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Union:} $L\cup M=\{x\mid x\in L\text{ or }x\in M\}$
  \item \textbf{Concatenation:} $LM=\{vw\mid v\in L,\;w\in M\}$
  \item \textbf{Kleene star:} $L^*=\bigcup_{i=0}^{\infty}L^i$, where
        $L^0=\{\varepsilon\}$ and $L^{i+1}=L^i\cdot L$
\end{itemize}
\end{defbox}

\subsection{Syntax and Semantics}

Regular expressions are built inductively.

\textbf{Base cases:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\emptyset$: the empty language; $L(\emptyset)=\emptyset$
  \item $\varepsilon$: the language $\{\varepsilon\}$
  \item $\mathbf{a}$ for any $a\in\Sigma$: the language $\{a\}$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step cases:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sum} $E+F$: $L(E+F)=L(E)\cup L(F)$
  \item \textbf{Concatenation} $EF$: $L(EF)=L(E)\,L(F)$
  \item \textbf{Kleene star} $E^*$: $L(E^*)=L(E)^*$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Operator precedence} (high to low): ${}^*\succ{\cdot}\succ{+}$.

So $01^*+1=\bigl((0\cdot(1^*))+1\bigr)$.

\subsection{Equivalence with DFAs and NFAs}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
  \toprule
  From  & To    & Method \\
  \midrule
  Regex & NFA   & Structural induction on the expression \\
  NFA   & DFA   & Power set construction \\
  DFA   & Regex & Kleene's algorithm (Section~10) \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\section{Kleene's Algorithm}
% ==============================================================================

Given a DFA $\dfa$ with states renamed $Q=\{1,\ldots,n\}$, Kleene's algorithm produces a
regular expression $R_\dfa$ with $L(\dfa)=L(R_\dfa)$.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{The key objects.}  For $0\leq k\leq n$ and $1\leq i,j\leq n$, let $R^{(k)}_{ij}$
be the regular expression describing all paths from state $i$ to state $j$ whose
\emph{intermediate} states are all numbered $\leq k$.

\medskip
\textbf{Base case ($k=0$):}  Only direct transitions allowed.
\[
  L^{(0)}_{ij}=
  \begin{cases}
    \{a\in\Sigma\mid\delta(i,a)=j\}
        & \text{if }i\neq j \\
    \{\varepsilon\}\cup\{a\in\Sigma\mid\delta(i,a)=j\}
        & \text{if }i=j
  \end{cases}
\]

\textbf{Inductive step ($k-1\mapsto k$):}
\[
  R^{(k)}_{ij}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{R^{(k-1)}_{ij}}_{\text{avoid }k}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{R^{(k-1)}_{ik}}_{\text{reach }k}
  \underbrace{\bigl(R^{(k-1)}_{kk}\bigr)^*}_{\text{loop at }k}
  \underbrace{R^{(k-1)}_{kj}}_{\text{leave }k}
\]

\textbf{Final result:}
\[
  R_\dfa\;=\;\sum_{q\in F}R^{(n)}_{q_0 q}
\]
\end{defbox}

\begin{insightbox}
\textbf{Note.}  This algorithm is correct but expensive: for $n$ states we compute
$O(n^3)$ expressions, each potentially of exponential length.  A more practical alternative
is the \textbf{state elimination method}.
\end{insightbox}

\section{DFA Minimization}
% ==============================================================================

Given a DFA, find the equivalent DFA with the \textbf{fewest states} by merging states that
are indistinguishable.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Definition (State Equivalence).}  States $p,q\in Q$ are \emph{equivalent},
written $p\sim q$, if:
\[
  \forall w\in\Sigma^*\colon
  \hatdelta(p,w)\in F\iff\hatdelta(q,w)\in F
\]
That is, no string can distinguish $p$ from $q$.
\end{defbox}

\subsection{The Table-Filling Method}

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=4pt]
  \item Mark all pairs $(p,q)$ where one is accepting and the other is not.
  \item For each unmarked pair $(p,q)$ and each $a\in\Sigma$: if
        $(\delta(p,a),\delta(q,a))$ is already marked, mark $(p,q)$ too.
  \item Repeat step~2 until no new pairs get marked.  Unmarked pairs are equivalent.
  \item Merge each equivalence class $[q]_\sim$ into a single state.  Remove
        unreachable states.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Construction (Minimised DFA).}
Define $\dfa^{\min}=(Q^{\min},\Sigma,\delta^{\min},q_0^{\min},F^{\min})$ by:
\begin{align*}
  Q^{\min}             &:=\{[q]_{\sim}\mid q\in Q\} \\
  \delta^{\min}([q],a) &:=[\delta(q,a)] \\
  q_0^{\min}           &:=[q_0] \\
  F^{\min}             &:=\{[q]\mid q\in F\}
\end{align*}
\end{defbox}

\begin{theorem}
Let $\dfa$ be a DFA with all states reachable.  Then $\dfa^{\min}$ is the (essentially
unique) DFA with the same language and the minimal number of states.
\end{theorem}

\section{The Pumping Lemma}
% ==============================================================================

Not every language is regular.  The Pumping Lemma lets us \emph{prove} a language is
\textbf{not} regular.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Theorem (Pumping Lemma).}  Let $L$ be a regular language.  Then there exists a
constant $n>0$ such that for every $w\in L$ with $|w|\geq n$, $w$ decomposes as $w=xyz$
satisfying:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $y\neq\varepsilon$
  \item $|xy|\leq n$
  \item $\forall k\geq 0\colon xy^kz\in L$
\end{enumerate}
\end{defbox}

\subsection{Why It Is True}

If $L=L(\dfa)$ for a DFA with $n$ states, any accepted word of length $\geq n$ forces $n+1$
states to be visited.  By the \textbf{Pigeonhole Principle} some state $p_i=p_j$ is visited
twice within the first $n+1$ steps.  The substring $y=a_{i+1}\cdots a_j$ forms a loop that
can be repeated (or skipped) any number of times.

\subsection{Proof Template}

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Assume} $L$ is regular for contradiction.  So $L$ has some pumping
        number~$n$.
  \item \textbf{Choose} a specific $w\in L$ with $|w|\geq n$ designed to force a
        contradiction.
  \item \textbf{Consider} all decompositions $w=xyz$ satisfying conditions~1 and~2.
  \item \textbf{Show} that for each split, some $xy^kz\notin L$, contradicting
        condition~3.
  \item \textbf{Conclude} $L$ is not regular.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{example}
Show that $L=\{a^nb^n\mid n\geq 0\}$ is not regular.

\begin{proof}
Assume $L$ is regular with pumping number $n$.  Choose $w=a^nb^n$ (so $|w|=2n\geq n$).
By condition~2, $xy\subseteq a^n$, so $y$ consists only of $a$'s.  By condition~1,
$|y|\geq 1$.  Pumping with $k=2$ gives $w'=xy^2z$, which has strictly more $a$'s than
$b$'s, so $w'\notin L$.  This contradicts condition~3.  Therefore $L$ is not regular.
\end{proof}
\end{example}

\begin{notebox}
\textbf{Important.}  The Pumping Lemma can only \emph{disprove} regularity.  Satisfying the
pumping conditions does not prove a language is regular --- for that you need to construct
a DFA or regular expression.
\end{notebox}

\section{Turing Machines \& Computability}
% ==============================================================================

DFAs cannot count arbitrarily or perform arithmetic.  Turing machines extend the model with
an infinite read/write tape.

\subsection{Why DFAs Are Insufficient}

Consider the language $L=\{10^n10^m10^{n+m}\mid n,m\in\mathbb{N}\}$, which encodes
addition.  This language is not regular (provable via the Pumping Lemma), so DFAs cannot
perform even basic arithmetic.  The core limitation: a DFA has \emph{finite} memory (finitely
many states), so it cannot store arbitrarily large numbers.

What is missing?  \textbf{Read/write memory.}  A Turing machine adds an infinite tape that
the machine can both read from and write to, and a head that moves in both directions.  This
gives it the power to compute anything that is algorithmically computable.

\subsection{Definition}

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Definition (Turing Machine).}  A TM is a 7-tuple
$M=(Q,\Sigma,\Gamma,\delta,q_0,B,F)$ where:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
  \item $Q$ --- finite set of states
  \item $\Sigma$ --- input alphabet
  \item $\Gamma$ --- tape alphabet, $\Sigma\subseteq\Gamma$
  \item $\delta(q,X)=(p,Y,D)$ --- in state $q$ reading $X$: go to $p$, write $Y$,
        move $D\in\{L,R\}$
  \item $q_0$ --- start state
  \item $B\in\Gamma\setminus\Sigma$ --- blank symbol
  \item $F\subseteq Q$ --- accepting states
\end{itemize}
\end{defbox}

\begin{theorem}
Every DFA can be regarded as a TM.  If $\delta(p,X)=q$ in the DFA, define
$\delta(p,X)=(q,X,R)$ in the TM.  When the TM reads the first blank, it halts.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Example TM: Accepting $\{0^n1^n\}$}

Strategy: match each $0$ to a $1$ by marking them $X$ and $Y$ respectively.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccccc}
  \toprule
  $\delta$ & $0$ & $1$ & $X$ & $Y$ & $B$ \\
  \midrule
  $q_0$ & $(q_1,X,R)$ & ---         & ---         & $(q_3,Y,R)$ & ---         \\
  $q_1$ & $(q_1,0,R)$ & $(q_2,Y,L)$ & ---         & $(q_1,Y,R)$ & ---         \\
  $q_2$ & ---         & ---         & $(q_0,X,R)$ & $(q_2,Y,L)$ & ---         \\
  $q_3$ & ---         & ---         & ---         & $(q_3,Y,R)$ & $(q_4,B,R)$ \\
  $q_4$ & \multicolumn{5}{c}{(accepting --- halt)} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Instantaneous Descriptions}

A configuration of a TM is written as an \textbf{ID}: tape contents with the current state
to the left of the scanned symbol.
\[
  q_0\,0011\;\vdash\;Xq_1\,011\;\vdash\;X0q_1\,11
  \;\vdash\;Xq_2\,0Y1\;\vdash\;\cdots
\]

\subsection{Computability Hierarchy}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
  \toprule
  Class & Definition & Example \\
  \midrule
  Regular              & Accepted by a DFA            & $\{w\mid w\text{ contains }01\}$ \\
  Recursively Enum.    & Accepted by some TM           & $\{0^n1^n\mid n\geq 0\}$ \\
  Recursive (Decidable)& TM that always halts          & Most computable problems \\
  Undecidable          & No TM can decide it           & The Halting Problem \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{insightbox}
\textbf{Key takeaway.}  Regular $\subset$ Recursive (Decidable) $\subset$ RE $\subset$
All Languages.  DFAs sit at the bottom; Turing machines reach RE\@.  But even TMs
cannot decide everything --- the Halting Problem (next section) is the most famous example.
\end{insightbox}

\section{The Halting Problem}
% ==============================================================================

The Halting Problem asks: given a program $P$ and an input $I$, does $P$ eventually
terminate on $I$?  We want to know whether a universal ``halt-checker'' can exist.

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{The Halting Problem --- Specification.}
Does there exist a program $H$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
  \item $H$ takes as input a program $P$ and an input $I$
  \item $H$ always terminates (it never loops forever)
  \item $H(P,I)=\texttt{true}$ if $P$ terminates on $I$
  \item $H(P,I)=\texttt{false}$ if $P$ does not terminate on $I$
\end{enumerate}
\end{defbox}

\begin{theorem}
The Halting Problem is undecidable.  No program $H$ satisfying all four properties above
can exist.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By contradiction.

\textbf{Step 1.}  Assume a program $H$ with all four properties exists.

\textbf{Step 2.}  Define a new ``strange'' program $S$ as follows:
\[
  S(X)\;:=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \text{loop forever} & \text{if }H(X,X)=\texttt{true} \\
    \text{halt}         & \text{if }H(X,X)=\texttt{false}
  \end{cases}
\]
In pseudo-code: \texttt{if H(X,X) then while true do skip}.

Note: since $H$ exists and always terminates, the definition of $S$ is well-formed.

\textbf{Step 3.}  Ask: what happens when we run $S(S)$?  We derive a chain of equivalences:
\begin{align*}
  H(S,S)=\texttt{true}
  &\iff S(S)\text{ terminates}  &&\text{(by definition of }H\text{)} \\
  &\iff H(S,S)=\texttt{false}  &&\text{(by definition of }S\text{)}
\end{align*}

This is a contradiction: $H(S,S)$ can be neither \texttt{true} nor \texttt{false}.

\textbf{Step 4.}  Therefore our assumption that $H$ exists is wrong.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Intuition --- Why Self-Reference Breaks Things}

The key trick is \textbf{diagonalisation} --- we feed the program to itself as input ($S$
applied to $S$).  This creates a self-referential paradox akin to ``this sentence is false.''
Any purported halt-checker $H$ can be weaponised against itself by constructing a program
that does the \emph{opposite} of what $H$ predicts.

\begin{notebox}
\textbf{Historical note.}  Turing (1937) introduced Turing machines precisely to formalise
the notion of ``program'' and prove this result.  The diagonalisation technique mirrors
Cantor's proof that the reals are uncountable.
\end{notebox}

% ==============================================================================
\section{Decidability Problems}
% ==============================================================================

A natural follow-up: which \emph{specific} questions about automata and Turing machines are
decidable or undecidable?  The answers differ dramatically depending on the model.

\subsection{Decidable Problems for DFAs}

Because DFAs have finite state spaces, many questions about them can be answered
algorithmically:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
  \toprule
  Problem      & Question          & Method \\
  \midrule
  Emptiness    & $L(\dfa)=\emptyset$?
               & Reachability: is any $q\in F$ reachable from $q_0$? \\
  Finiteness   & $|L(\dfa)|<\infty$?
               & Check for cycles on paths from $q_0$ to $F$ \\
  Membership   & $w\in L(\dfa)$?
               & Simulate $\dfa$ on $w$ --- $O(|w|)$ time \\
  Equivalence  & $L(\dfa_1)=L(\dfa_2)$?
               & Minimise both and check isomorphism \\
  Universality & $L(\dfa)=\Sigma^*$?
               & Complement $\dfa$ and test emptiness \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{insightbox}
All of the above are decidable because DFAs have a \textbf{finite} number of states --- we
can exhaustively explore the entire state space.
\end{insightbox}

\subsection{Undecidable Problems for Turing Machines}

For Turing machines, almost every non-trivial question becomes undecidable.  These can be
shown undecidable by \textbf{reduction} from the Halting Problem.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llc}
  \toprule
  Problem     & Question                         & Decidable? \\
  \midrule
  Halting     & Does TM $M$ halt on input $w$?   & \textcolor{red}{No} \\
  Emptiness   & $L(M)=\emptyset$?                & \textcolor{red}{No} \\
  Equivalence & $L(M_1)=L(M_2)$?                 & \textcolor{red}{No} \\
  Regularity  & Is $L(M)$ regular?               & \textcolor{red}{No} \\
  Membership  & $w\in L(M)$?  (in general)       & \textcolor{red}{No} \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{The Sharp Contrast}

\begin{defbox}
\textbf{Key Insight.}  The \emph{same question} can be decidable for DFAs but undecidable
for TMs.  The difference: DFAs have \textbf{bounded} computation (finite states, one pass, no
writing), while TMs have \textbf{unbounded} computation (infinite tape, arbitrary rewinding).
The power that makes TMs universal also makes them unpredictable.
\end{defbox}

\subsection{Rice's Theorem (Conceptual)}

Any \emph{non-trivial} property of the language recognised by a Turing machine is
undecidable.  A property is ``non-trivial'' if some TMs have it and some do not.  This means
questions like ``does this TM accept at least one string?'' or ``is the language of this TM
finite?'' are all undecidable.

\begin{notebox}
\textbf{For the midterm.}  Remember the sharp contrast --- decidable for DFAs, undecidable
for TMs.  The halting problem proof by contradiction (assume $H$ exists $\to$ build strange
program $S$ $\to$ derive $S(S)$ paradox) is the key proof technique.
\end{notebox}

\paragraph{Exercise 2.2.7}
Let $A$ be a DFA and let $q$ be a state such that $\delta(q,a)=q$ for all input symbols $a\in\Sigma$.
We prove by induction on $|w|$ that for all strings $w\in\Sigma^*$, $\hat{\delta}(q,w)=q$.

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Base case:} $|w|=0$, so $w=\varepsilon$.
By definition of $\hat{\delta}$, $\hat{\delta}(q,\varepsilon)=q$.

\textbf{Inductive step:} Assume that for some $n\ge 0$, for every string $x\in\Sigma^*$ with $|x|=n$ we have $\hat{\delta}(q,x)=q$.
Let $w\in\Sigma^*$ be any string with $|w|=n+1$.
Then $w$ can be written as $w=xa$ where $|x|=n$ and $a\in\Sigma$ is the last symbol.
Using the recursive definition of $\hat{\delta}$,
\[
\hat{\delta}(q,xa)=\delta(\hat{\delta}(q,x),a).
\]
By the inductive hypothesis, $\hat{\delta}(q,x)=q$, so
\[
\hat{\delta}(q,xa)=\delta(q,a)=q,
\]
where the last equality uses the assumption that $\delta(q,a)=q$ for all $a\in\Sigma$.
Thus $\hat{\delta}(q,w)=q$ for all $w$ of length $n+1$.

By induction, $\hat{\delta}(q,w)=q$ for all $w\in\Sigma^*$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Exercise 1 -- Kleene's Algorithm}

Consider the DFA with states $p$ and $q$, where $p$ is the start and accepting state, and the transitions are:
\[
p \xrightarrow{a} p,\quad p \xrightarrow{b} q,\quad q \xrightarrow{a} p,\quad q \xrightarrow{b} q.
\]
Let $1=p$ and $2=q$.

For Kleene's algorithm, the base regular expressions are:
\[
R_{11}^{(0)}=\varepsilon + a,\qquad
R_{12}^{(0)}=b,\qquad
R_{21}^{(0)}=a,\qquad
R_{22}^{(0)}=\varepsilon + b.
\]

Using the recurrence
\[
R_{ij}^{(k)} = R_{ij}^{(k-1)} + R_{ik}^{(k-1)}\left(R_{kk}^{(k-1)}\right)^*R_{kj}^{(k-1)},
\]
we first compute the $k=1$ stage (eliminating state 1):
\begin{align*}
R_{11}^{(1)} &= (\varepsilon + a) + (\varepsilon + a)(\varepsilon + a)^*(\varepsilon + a) = a^* \\
R_{12}^{(1)} &= b + (\varepsilon + a)(\varepsilon + a)^* b = a^*b \\
R_{21}^{(1)} &= a + (\varepsilon + b)(\varepsilon + a)^* a = aa^* = a^+ \\
R_{22}^{(1)} &= (\varepsilon + b) + (\varepsilon + b)(\varepsilon + a)^* b = \varepsilon + a^*b
\end{align*}

Then the $k=2$ stage (eliminating state 2) gives the language expression $R_{11}^{(2)}$, since state 1 ($=p$) is the only accepting state:
\begin{align*}
R_{11}^{(2)} &= R_{11}^{(1)} + R_{12}^{(1)}\left(R_{22}^{(1)}\right)^* R_{21}^{(1)} \\
             &= a^* + a^*b\,(\varepsilon + a^*b)^*\,a^+ \\
             &= a^* + a^*b(a^*b)^*a^+
\end{align*}

This simplifies to the regular expression:
\[
R = \varepsilon + (a+b)^*a.
\]

Thus, the language accepted by the DFA is:
\[
L(A)=L(R)=\{\varepsilon\}\cup\{w\in\{a,b\}^* : w \text{ ends in } a\}.
\]

\subsection{Week 5}

\subsubsection*{Pumping Lemma Exercises}

We use the pumping lemma to show that each of the following languages is not regular. Recall that the pumping lemma states that if a language $L$ is regular, then there exists a pumping length $p$ such that any string $s \in L$ with $|s| \ge p$ can be written as $s = xyz$ where:

\begin{itemize}
\item $|xy| \le p$
\item $|y| > 0$
\item $xy^iz \in L$ for all $i \ge 0$
\end{itemize}

We will assume each language is regular and derive a contradiction.

\paragraph{$L_1 = \{a^n b^m \mid n,m \in \mathbb{N}, n > m \ge 0\}$}

Assume $L_1$ is regular and let $p$ be the pumping length.  
Choose the string:

\[
s = a^{p+1}b^p
\]

Clearly $s \in L_1$ because $n=p+1$ and $m=p$ so $n>m$.

By the pumping lemma, $s = xyz$ where $|xy| \le p$ and $|y|>0$. Since the first $p$ characters are all $a$'s, the substring $y$ must consist only of $a$'s.

Let $y = a^k$ where $k>0$.

Now pump down with $i=0$:

\[
xz = a^{p+1-k}b^p
\]

Now the number of $a$'s is $p+1-k$ which is $\le p$. This means the number of $a$'s is no longer greater than the number of $b$'s. Therefore $xz \notin L_1$.

This contradicts the pumping lemma, so $L_1$ is not regular.

\paragraph{$L_2 = \{a^{n^2} \mid n \ge 1\}$}

Assume $L_2$ is regular and let $p$ be the pumping length.

Choose the string:

\[
s = a^{p^2}
\]

Since $p^2$ is a perfect square, $s \in L_2$.

Because $|xy| \le p$, the substring $y$ consists only of $a$'s. Let $y = a^k$ with $k>0$.

Pump the string with $i=2$:

\[
xy^2z = a^{p^2 + k}
\]

However, $p^2 + k$ is not guaranteed to be a perfect square since $1 \le k \le p$. Therefore the pumped string is not of the form $a^{n^2}$.

Thus $xy^2z \notin L_2$, contradicting the pumping lemma. Therefore $L_2$ is not regular.

\paragraph{$L_3 = \{a^p \mid p \text{ is prime}\}$}

Assume $L_3$ is regular and let $p$ be the pumping length.

Choose a prime number $q > p$ and let:

\[
s = a^q
\]

Clearly $s \in L_3$.

Again $|xy| \le p$ so $y = a^k$ for some $k>0$.

Pump the string with $i=q+1$:

\[
xy^{q+1}z = a^{q + qk}
\]

The exponent becomes:

\[
q + qk = q(1+k)
\]

This number is composite since it has factors $q$ and $(1+k)$ with $k \ge 1$. Therefore the pumped string is not prime length.

Thus $xy^{q+1}z \notin L_3$, contradicting the pumping lemma. Therefore $L_3$ is not regular.

\paragraph{$L_4 = \{a^k b^m a^{k+m} \mid k,m \ge 0\}$}

Assume $L_4$ is regular and let $p$ be the pumping length.

Choose:

\[
s = a^p b^p a^{2p}
\]

This string belongs to $L_4$ since $k=p$ and $m=p$, so the final block has length $k+m = 2p$.

Because $|xy| \le p$, the substring $y$ lies entirely within the first block of $a$'s.

Let $y = a^j$ where $j>0$.

Pump down with $i=0$:

\[
xz = a^{p-j} b^p a^{2p}
\]

Now the first block has $p-j$ $a$'s while the final block still has $2p$ $a$'s. 
However the language requires the final block to have exactly $k+m$ $a$'s, 
which should now be:

\[
(p-j) + p = 2p - j
\]

But the final block still has $2p$ $a$'s, so the string no longer satisfies the language definition.

Thus $xz \notin L_4$, contradicting the pumping lemma. Therefore $L_4$ is not regular.

\subparagraph{1. Unary addition: $10^n10^m \rightarrow 10^n10^m10^{n+m}$}

The assignment requires the output to \emph{append} a third block $10^{n+m}$ after the original input, leaving $10^n10^m$ intact.

\textbf{Idea:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Use two passes. In the first pass, scan the input to count the total number of $0$'s (there are $n+m$ of them).

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{True.} Given deciders $M_1$ for $L_1$ and $M_2$ for $L_2$, construct a TM $M$ that on input $w$: runs $M_1$ on $w$; if $M_1$ accepts, accept; otherwise run $M_2$ on $w$ and return its answer. Both $M_1$ and $M_2$ always halt, so $M$ always halts. $M$ accepts $w$ iff $w\in L_1$ or $w\in L_2$, i.e.\ iff $w\in L_1\cup L_2$.

    \item \textbf{True.} Given a decider $M$ for $L$, construct $\overline{M}$ that runs $M$ and swaps accept/reject. Since $M$ always halts, so does $\overline{M}$, and it accepts exactly the strings not in $L$, i.e.\ $\overline{L}$.

    \item \textbf{True.} $\varepsilon\in L^*$ always, so accept immediately on empty input. For a non-empty input $w$, try all $2^{|w|-1}$ ways to partition $w = w_1 w_2 \cdots w_k$ and for each partition run the decider for $L$ on every piece. Accept if some partition has every piece in $L$; reject otherwise. There are finitely many partitions and the decider for $L$ always halts, so this procedure always terminates.

    \item \textbf{True.} Given recognizers $M_1$ for $L_1$ and $M_2$ for $L_2$, construct a TM $M$ that on input $w$ \emph{dovetails} (interleaves) the simulations: run $M_1$ for one step, then $M_2$ for one step, then $M_1$ for two steps, and so on. If either machine ever accepts, $M$ accepts. Since any $w\in L_1\cup L_2$ is accepted by at least one of $M_1, M_2$ in finitely many steps, $M$ will eventually accept it. Hence $L_1\cup L_2$ is r.e.

    \item \textbf{False.} The halting language $\mathit{HALT} = \{(M,w)\mid M \text{ halts on } w\}$ is r.e.\ (simulate $M$ on $w$; accept if it halts) but its complement $\overline{\mathit{HALT}}$ is not r.e.\ This is a standard result: if both $\mathit{HALT}$ and $\overline{\mathit{HALT}}$ were r.e., then $\mathit{HALT}$ would be decidable, contradicting the undecidability of the halting problem.

    \item \textbf{True.} Given a recognizer $M$ for $L$, we build a recognizer for $L^*$. On input $w$, enumerate all partitions of $w$ into non-empty pieces and dovetail the simulation of $M$ on each piece across all partitions simultaneously. Accept as soon as all pieces in some partition are accepted by $M$. Since any $w\in L^*$ has a valid decomposition into words of $L$, each accepted in finite time, the machine will eventually find and confirm that partition.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Exercise 2 -- Problems on Decidability}

We classify each language as decidable, recursively enumerable (r.e.), or co-recursively enumerable (co-r.e.).

\subparagraph{1. $L_1 := \{\, M \mid M \text{ halts on itself} \,\}$}

This language is \textbf{recursively enumerable}, but \textbf{not decidable}.

\textbf{Why it is r.e.:}
Given a Turing machine $M$, we can simulate $M$ on its own encoding $\langle M \rangle$.
If $M$ eventually halts on $\langle M \rangle$, then our simulator will also halt and accept.
If $M$ never halts on $\langle M \rangle$, then the simulator runs forever.
Therefore $L_1$ is recursively enumerable.

\textbf{Why it is not decidable:}
This is a version of the halting problem.
If we could decide whether $M$ halts on itself, then we would be able to solve a halting-type problem algorithmically, which is impossible.
Thus $L_1$ is undecidable.

\textbf{About the complement:}
Since $L_1$ is r.e.\ but not decidable, its complement cannot also be r.e.
Otherwise both $L_1$ and $\overline{L_1}$ would be r.e., which would imply that $L_1$ is decidable.
So $\overline{L_1}$ is \textbf{not} r.e., meaning $L_1$ is \textbf{not co-r.e.}

\[
\boxed{L_1 \text{ is r.e.\ but neither decidable nor co-r.e.}}
\]

\subparagraph{2. $L_2 := \{\, (M,w) \mid M \text{ halts on the word } w \,\}$}

This language is also \textbf{recursively enumerable}, but \textbf{not decidable}.

\textbf{Why it is r.e.:}
Given $(M,w)$, simulate $M$ on input $w$.
If $M$ halts, accept.
If $M$ does not halt, the simulation continues forever.
Hence $L_2$ is recursively enumerable.

\textbf{Why it is not decidable:}
This is exactly the standard halting problem, which is known to be undecidable.

\textbf{About the complement:}
For the same reason as above, $\overline{L_2}$ is not recursively enumerable.
If both $L_2$ and $\overline{L_2}$ were r.e., then $L_2$ would be decidable, contradicting undecidability.

\[
\boxed{L_2 \text{ is r.e.\ but neither decidable nor co-r.e.}}
\]

\subparagraph{3. $L_3 := \{\, (M,w,k) \mid M \text{ halts on } w \text{ in at most } k \text{ steps} \,\}$}

This language is \textbf{decidable}.

\textbf{Decision procedure:}
Given $(M,w,k)$, simulate $M$ on input $w$ for exactly $k$ steps.
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $M$ halts within those $k$ steps, accept.
    \item If it has not halted after $k$ steps, reject.
\end{itemize}

This always terminates, because $k$ is a fixed finite bound.
Therefore $L_3$ is decidable.

Since every decidable language is also both r.e.\ and co-r.e., we conclude:

\[
\boxed{L_3 \text{ is decidable (and therefore also r.e.\ and co-r.e.)}}
\]

\paragraph{Exercise 4 -- Landau Notation}

We compare the following asymptotic classes.

\subparagraph{1. $\mathcal{O}(n)$ and $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{n})$}

Since $\sqrt{n} \le n$ for all $n \ge 1$, every function in $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{n})$ is also in $\mathcal{O}(n)$.
However, not every linear function is bounded by a constant multiple of $\sqrt{n}$.

\[
\boxed{\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{n}) \subsetneq \mathcal{O}(n)}
\]

\subparagraph{2. $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ and $\mathcal{O}(2^n)$}

Exponential growth dominates polynomial growth.
In particular, $n^2 = O(2^n)$, but $2^n \notin O(n^2)$.

\[
\boxed{\mathcal{O}(n^2) \subsetneq \mathcal{O}(2^n)}
\]

\subparagraph{3. $\mathcal{O}(\log n)$ and $\mathcal{O}(\log n \cdot \log n)$}

We have $\log n \le (\log n)^2$ for all sufficiently large $n$, so every function in $\mathcal{O}(\log n)$ is also in $\mathcal{O}((\log n)^2)$.
The reverse inclusion does not hold in general.

\[
\boxed{\mathcal{O}(\log n) \subsetneq \mathcal{O}((\log n)^2)}
\]

\subparagraph{4. $\mathcal{O}(2^n)$ and $\mathcal{O}(3^n)$}

Because $2^n \le 3^n$ for all $n$, every function in $\mathcal{O}(2^n)$ is also in $\mathcal{O}(3^n)$.
But $3^n$ grows strictly faster than $2^n$, so the inclusion is proper.

\[
\boxed{\mathcal{O}(2^n) \subsetneq \mathcal{O}(3^n)}
\]

\subparagraph{5. $\mathcal{O}(\log_2 n)$ and $\mathcal{O}(\log_3 n)$}

By the change-of-base formula,
\[
\log_2 n = \frac{\log_3 n}{\log_3 2}.
\]
Thus $\log_2 n$ and $\log_3 n$ differ only by a constant factor, so they define the same asymptotic class.

\[
\boxed{\mathcal{O}(\log_2 n) = \mathcal{O}(\log_3 n)}
\]
